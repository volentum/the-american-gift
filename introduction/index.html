Introduction

Three Days in a Rowboat

From The American Gift · by Eduardo G. Perez, Jr.

November 25, 1964. The day before Thanksgiving. While American families were preparing to celebrate abundance and gratitude, a 24-year-old Cuban man was stealing a rowboat and betting his life on three words: "Land of the Free."

That man was my father, Eduardo G. Perez, Sr. And his decision to paddle for three days through shark-infested waters didn't just change his life – it created mine.

More importantly for you, it created the perspective that makes this book necessary.

The Theft That Changed Everything

My father didn't leave Cuba because he was poor. He left because he was smart enough to recognize what was coming and courageous enough to risk everything to escape it.

In pre-Castro Cuba, he had been somebody. Position. Respect. A future. He was building something meaningful in the country where he was born, surrounded by the culture that had shaped him, speaking the language that felt like home.

But Fidel Castro's revolution wasn't just changing Cuba's government – it was destroying Cuba's soul. The entrepreneurship that had built my father's opportunities was being criminalized. The freedom that had enabled his dreams was being systematically eliminated. The future he had planned for his family was being confiscated by an ideology that saw individual success as a threat to collective control.

So my father made a choice that defines everything I believe about America, business, and the mortgage industry: he chose opportunity over comfort, freedom over security, and his children's future over his own present.

He stole a rowboat – literally committed theft – because staying legal meant staying trapped.

Three Days Through Hell

I've tried to imagine what those three days were like. The exhaustion. The dehydration. The certainty that every shadow in the water might be a shark. The growing doubt about whether the boat would hold together, whether his strength would last, whether the dream of America was worth the reality of potentially dying in the middle of the ocean.

My father never talked much about the journey itself. But he talked constantly about what it meant.

"I didn't steal a boat and paddle for three days so you could play small," he would tell me when I was tempted to take shortcuts or settle for "good enough." "I didn't risk everything so my son wouldn't risk anything."

That lesson – that the American Gift demands everything from those who would claim it – shaped every decision I've made in business and life.

The Homeless Week

When my father finally reached American shores, he had nothing except determination and the clothes on his back. He spent his first week in America homeless, sleeping wherever he could find shelter, surviving on whatever charity was available.

But he wasn't bitter. He wasn't angry. He wasn't even particularly worried.

He was grateful.

"In Cuba, I had position but no freedom," he would explain years later. "In America, I had freedom but no position. I knew which one I could build from."

That first week taught him something that shaped how our family would approach every challenge: freedom without comfort is still freedom. Position without freedom isn't position at all – it's just a comfortable prison.

The American Gift Revelation

Here's where my father's story intersects with the mortgage industry crisis you're about to read about.

People on the outside call it the American Dream. But my father understood something that most Americans – and certainly most American business leaders – have forgotten: once you're here, once you've been given access to American opportunities, it stops being a dream and becomes a gift.

And gifts create obligations.

The American Gift isn't just about what you can achieve for yourself. It's about what you're obligated to achieve for others. It's not just about building wealth – it's about building systems that create wealth for people who come after you.

The mortgage industry has forgotten this distinction. The Old Guard treats American homeownership as a profit center to be optimized rather than a gift to be shared. They've turned the most important wealth-building transaction most Americans will ever make into a wealth extraction machine that benefits everyone except the families trying to achieve the American Dream.

This isn't just bad business. It's a betrayal of everything America represents.

The Rowboat To Private Jet Story

By any measure, my father succeeded in America. From that homeless week in 1964 to building multiple successful businesses. From stealing a rowboat to watching his son buy private jets. From not speaking English to raising a son who would serve on the boards of national industry associations.

One generation. Rowboat to private jet. That's the American Gift in action.

But here's what drives me crazy about how the mortgage industry operates today: they've made it harder for the next Eduardo Perez, Sr. to achieve what my father achieved. They've created barriers, fees, complications, and regulatory requirements that serve their own interests while making homeownership – the foundation of American wealth building – more expensive and less accessible.

They're stealing from the American Gift to fund their own comfortable lives.

The Obligation That Created This Book

I didn't write this book to attack the mortgage industry. I wrote it to save the mortgage industry from itself.

The mortgage industry is supposed to be the engine of the American Dream. We facilitate homeownership – the primary way American families build generational wealth. We handle trillions of dollars in transactions that determine whether people become renters or owners, tenants or stakeholders, dreamers or achievers.

That's not just a business opportunity. That's a sacred responsibility.

But somewhere along the way, too many people in this industry forgot that we exist to serve the American Gift, not to profit from it. They started optimizing for their own wealth extraction rather than their clients' wealth creation. They began treating mortgage transactions as profit opportunities rather than dream fulfillment processes.

My father didn't steal a boat and paddle for three days so I could participate in that betrayal.

The Perfect Storm Metaphor

They call me "The Perfect Storm" – El Niño – because I bring disruption wherever I go. But storms aren't just destructive forces. They're also cleansing forces. They wash away what's rotten and create space for new growth.

The mortgage industry needs that kind of storm right now.

The Old Guard's business models are built on consumer ignorance, regulatory capture, and artificial scarcities. They depend on keeping borrowers confused, competitors divided, and regulators captured. They've turned complexity into a competitive advantage and transparency into a threat.

But perfect storms have a way of exposing what's really going on beneath the surface. They strip away the superficial and reveal the structural. They force everyone to focus on what really matters versus what just appears to matter.

That's what this book is designed to do.

The Three Audiences

I'm writing this book for three distinct audiences, and I want to be clear about what each group should expect.

For Consumers: You're about to learn why your mortgage costs thousands more than it should, takes longer than it needs to, and involves more complexity than it requires. More importantly, you're going to understand what you can do about it.

For Mortgage Professionals: You're about to see your industry through the eyes of someone who loves what we do but refuses to accept how we've been doing it. This book will either inspire you to be part of the solution or expose you as part of the problem.

For Industry Leaders: You're about to be held accountable for what you've built and what you've allowed to persist. Some of you will recognize the validity of these criticisms and work to address them. Others will dismiss them and continue business as usual. History will judge which choice you make.

The Structure Of Revelation

This book is organized to take you through the complete story of what's wrong with the mortgage industry and how to fix it.

Part I exposes the Old Guard's reign of terror – the specific ways they've corrupted American homeownership for their own benefit.

Part II introduces the philosophical foundation of the New Guard – the principles and perspectives that drive genuine industry reform.

Part III shows you the New Guard in action – the specific strategies and tactics that are already transforming how mortgages work.

Part IV explores what it means to operate in the Eye of the Storm – the calm center where real leadership happens.

Part V paints a picture of what victory looks like – how the mortgage industry can fulfill its promise to serve the American Dream.

The Warning And The Promise

Let me be clear about what you're getting into by reading this book.

This isn't a polite critique of industry practices. This isn't a measured analysis of regulatory policy. This isn't a diplomatic suggestion for incremental improvement.

This is a declaration of war against business practices that betray the American Dream. This is an expose of industry leaders who have confused wealth extraction with value creation. This is a manifesto for revolutionary change in how America finances homeownership.

Some people in the mortgage industry are going to hate this book. They're going to attack my credibility, question my motives, and dismiss my arguments. They're going to claim I'm an outsider who doesn't understand how things really work.

They're wrong on all counts, but they're going to try anyway.

But here's my promise to you: every claim in this book is backed by evidence. Every criticism is supported by facts. Every solution I propose is based on real-world experience serving real families trying to achieve real homeownership.

I'm not writing from theory. I'm writing from the trenches of an industry I've spent two decades learning, serving, and ultimately deciding to reform.

The Generational Responsibility

My father's generation fled countries where individual success was criminalized and entrepreneurship was treated as exploitation. They came to America because this was the place where hard work, smart decisions, and ethical business practices could create generational wealth.

My generation has an obligation to ensure that America remains that place.

The mortgage industry's current trajectory threatens that promise. When an industry that should enable wealth creation becomes primarily focused on wealth extraction, when the process of achieving homeownership becomes more expensive and complicated than necessary, when regulatory capture allows established players to eliminate competition – America stops being the land of opportunity and becomes just another place where the powerful exploit the powerless.

I refuse to let that happen on my watch.

The Father's Legacy

My father died before he could see me build EPM into an Inc. 5000 company. He didn't witness my service as Chairman of MORPAC or my appointment to the MBA Board. He never saw the industry recognition or business success that his sacrifice made possible.

But I know exactly what he would think of this book.

He would ask whether I was honoring The Code of Life he taught me. Whether I was using my position to serve the American Gift or just to enrich myself. Whether I was leaving my fellow man better than I found him.

This book is my answer to those questions.

The Invitation

What you're about to read will change how you think about the mortgage industry, homeownership in America, and the relationship between business success and social responsibility.

You'll discover that many of the problems with American homeownership aren't accidents or unintended consequences – they're deliberate business strategies designed to maximize industry profits at consumer expense.

You'll learn that the regulatory system created after the 2008 crisis was designed more to protect established industry players than to protect consumers.

You'll understand that the complexity, cost, and delays that characterize modern mortgage transactions aren't necessary features of responsible lending – they're artificial barriers created to reduce competition and increase profitability.

Most importantly, you'll see that change isn't just possible – it's inevitable. The Perfect Storm is already here. The only question is whether you'll be part of the destruction or part of the rebuilding.

The Promise Of America

My father stole a rowboat in 1964 because he believed in the promise of America – that this is a place where courage, hard work, and ethical behavior can create opportunities that don't exist anywhere else in the world.

Sixty years later, I'm writing this book because I still believe in that promise.

But promises require vigilance. They demand that each generation recommit to the principles and practices that make them possible. They need people who are willing to fight for them when they're threatened by greed, corruption, or simple complacency.

The mortgage industry is at a crossroads. We can continue down the path of wealth extraction, regulatory capture, and consumer exploitation that has characterized the Old Guard's approach. Or we can recommit to our role as servants of the American Dream, facilitators of homeownership, and guardians of the promise that made my father's sacrifice meaningful.

I know which path I'm choosing.

The question is: which path will you choose?
